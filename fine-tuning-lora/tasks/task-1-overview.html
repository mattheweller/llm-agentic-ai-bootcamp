What Is Fine-Tuning?

Fine-tuning means continuing the training process of a pre-trained model on your own labelled dataset. The model already understands language — you're teaching it the specific patterns, vocabulary, and output format for your task.

Think of it like hiring an experienced lawyer and sending them through a one-week onboarding for your firm's specific legal practice areas. They don't need to learn what a contract is — they need to learn your contracts.

Key Insight: Fine-tuning doesn't change what the model knows about language. It changes the distribution of outputs it produces — steering responses towards the task-specific format and vocabulary you need.

When to Fine-Tune vs. Other Approaches

Before reaching for fine-tuning, ask yourself whether a simpler approach covers the use case:

Approach Best For Limitations Cost
Prompting General tasks, rapid prototyping, varied queries Inconsistent format, token-heavy, no task-specific adaptation Low setup, higher per-call cost
RAG Grounding answers in a private knowledge base Doesn't change model behaviour or output style Medium — needs a vector store
Fine-Tuning Consistent format, domain-specific style, classification tasks Requires labelled data; training compute; periodic retraining Higher upfront, low inference cost
Rule of Thumb: Try prompting first. If you're seeing consistent format failures, hallucinated labels, or unacceptable latency from long system prompts, fine-tuning is the right next step.

Why Open-Source Models?

Proprietary API models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) are excellent for general tasks, but production fine-tuning often uses open-source models. Here's why:

Data Privacy

Financial data, medical records, and proprietary business data can't leave your infrastructure. Open-source models run entirely on your hardware.

Cost at Scale

Once trained, inference on a small fine-tuned model costs a fraction of a GPT-4 API call — critical when processing millions of documents.

Full Control

You choose the model version, quantization level, and serving infrastructure. No vendor lock-in, no surprise deprecations.

Specialization

A fine-tuned 1B model often outperforms a general 70B model on a narrow task — smaller, faster, and more accurate where it matters.

The Project: Financial News Sentiment Classification

Throughout this module, you'll fine-tune Google Gemma 3 1B to classify financial news headlines into three sentiment categories:

positive negative neutral

Example inputs and expected outputs:

News Headline Label
"Company reports record quarterly earnings, beating analyst expectations by 15%" positive
"Supply chain disruptions expected to impact margins through Q3" negative
"Board of directors approves share buyback programme of up to €500m" neutral

Why This Task Benefits from Fine-Tuning

General-purpose models struggle with financial sentiment because the domain has non-obvious language patterns:

  • "The company will acquire its competitor" — neutral to a general model, but market-moving in finance
  • "Operating costs rose 8% year-over-year" — sounds negative, but may be expected and priced in
  • Short, terse headlines with no explicit sentiment cues require domain context

A fine-tuned model learns these domain-specific patterns from labelled examples, achieving ~88% accuracy vs. ~44% for zero-shot prompting.

What You'll Build: A sentiment classification pipeline that loads financial news from Hugging Face, formats it for supervised fine-tuning, trains Gemma 3 1B with LoRA adapters, and evaluates the improvement quantitatively.
